## What It Means

**Feedback linearization** is a nonlinear topic in the **Control Methods** branch of the control-theory map. In this context, it focuses on state-dependent dynamics, local versus global behavior, Lyapunov arguments, and model-based cancellation or switching. It belongs to controller design methods that turn models, measurements, and objectives into feedback or feedforward actions, so the important question is not only what the method computes, but what assumptions make the computation meaningful for a closed loop.

A useful way to read this topic is as a connection between three objects: a model of the plant or signal, an objective stated in engineering terms, and evidence that the resulting behavior is stable, accurate, robust, or safe enough for the application.

## Mathematical Form

A useful control-theory abstraction is a dynamical map with an objective:

$$\dot x=f(x,u,d,t),\qquad y=h(x,u,t),\qquad e=r-y.$$

The notation should be interpreted with the page topic in mind: $x$ is the state or configuration, $u$ is the manipulated input, $y$ is the measured output, $r$ is a reference, $e$ is an error signal, and disturbances or modeling errors are included when they materially affect the design.

## Intuition

The key intuition is to ask what information this topic adds to the feedback loop. Some topics add a model, some add a controller, some add an estimator, and some add a test. For **Feedback linearization**, the central issue is the behavior summarized above: cancels nonlinearities through feedback to obtain a simpler closed-loop form. The method still has to remain consistent with the plant assumptions and the performance objective.

A practical example is a robot or converter operated near constraints: a certificate or nonlinear design argument proves convergence or forward invariance in a region where linear intuition alone is not enough.

## How It Is Used

1. Specify the signals, units, sampling convention, operating point, and uncertainty assumptions.
2. Choose the representation that matches the topic: transfer function, state-space model, graph, stochastic model, optimization problem, or certificate.
3. Apply the method to obtain a controller, estimate, trajectory, reduced model, stability statement, or diagnostic quantity.
4. Check the result against the original engineering requirement, including actuator limits, disturbances, noise, and implementation constraints.
5. Validate with an independent calculation, simulation, experiment, or residual check before relying on the conclusion.

## Common Pitfalls

- Check that the linear model is valid over the amplitudes and operating points used in the design.
- State the region of validity; local, regional, and global claims are not interchangeable.
