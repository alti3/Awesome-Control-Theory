## What It Means

**Trajectory tracking** is a tracking topic in the **State Estimation** branch of the control-theory map. In this context, it focuses on maintaining estimates of moving objects, references, or trajectories over time. It belongs to estimation methods that infer unmeasured states, parameters, disturbances, maps, or tracks from models and data, so the important question is not only what the method computes, but what assumptions make the computation meaningful for a closed loop.

A useful way to read this topic is as a connection between three objects: a model of the plant or signal, an objective stated in engineering terms, and evidence that the resulting behavior is stable, accurate, robust, or safe enough for the application.

## Mathematical Form

The estimator balances model prediction and measurement correction:

$$
\hat x_k^+=\hat x_k^-+K_k\bigl(y_k-C\hat x_k^-\bigr).
$$

The notation should be interpreted with the page topic in mind: $x$ is the state or configuration, $u$ is the manipulated input, $y$ is the measured output, $r$ is a reference, $e$ is an error signal, and disturbances or modeling errors are included when they materially affect the design.

## Intuition

The key intuition is to ask what information this topic adds to the feedback loop. Some topics add a model, some add a controller, some add an estimator, and some add a test. For **Trajectory tracking**, the central issue is the behavior summarized above: estimates deviation from a desired path or reference. The method still has to remain consistent with the plant assumptions and the performance objective.

A practical example is vehicle navigation: an inertial model predicts motion between updates, while GPS, camera, wheel, or range measurements correct drift and expose outliers through residuals.

The Starship booster catch is a high-energy example of trajectory tracking as part of a larger control stack. The planned descent trajectory has to be followed through hypersonic re-entry, aerodynamic drag, turbulence, changing attitude, and tight timing near the catch arms. The tracking loop depends on state estimation from fused sensors, then uses aerodynamic surfaces and propulsion to reject disturbances and correct path error in real time. The example also shows why trajectory tracking is not just "following a line": it couples estimation, nonlinear dynamics, actuator limits, disturbance rejection, and terminal accuracy.

## How It Is Used

1. Specify the signals, units, sampling convention, operating point, and uncertainty assumptions.
2. Choose the representation that matches the topic: transfer function, state-space model, graph, stochastic model, optimization problem, or certificate.
3. Apply the method to obtain a controller, estimate, trajectory, reduced model, stability statement, or diagnostic quantity.
4. Check the result against the original engineering requirement, including actuator limits, disturbances, noise, and implementation constraints.
5. Validate with an independent calculation, simulation, experiment, or residual check before relying on the conclusion.

## Common Pitfalls

- Check that the linear model is valid over the amplitudes and operating points used in the design.
- Validate noise covariances and residuals; a filter can look smooth while becoming statistically inconsistent.
- Test solver convergence, infeasibility handling, scaling, and warm starts at the intended real-time rate.
