# Safety constraints

Define forbidden states, operating envelopes, and acceptable risk.

## What It Means

**Safety constraints** is a foundational tools topic in the **First Principles & Classical Tools** branch of the control-theory map. In this context, it focuses on the part of the control workflow described by this map category. It belongs to physical and classical modeling tools that connect engineering assumptions to mathematical control models, so the important question is not only what the method computes, but what assumptions make the computation meaningful for a closed loop.

A useful way to read this topic is as a connection between three objects: a model of the plant or signal, an objective stated in engineering terms, and evidence that the resulting behavior is stable, accurate, robust, or safe enough for the application.

## Mathematical Form

Many stability and safety arguments reduce to a certificate inequality:

$$V(x)>0,\qquad \dot V(x)=\nabla V(x)^T f(x,u)\le 0.$$

The notation should be interpreted with the page topic in mind: $x$ is the state or configuration, $u$ is the manipulated input, $y$ is the measured output, $r$ is a reference, $e$ is an error signal, and disturbances or modeling errors are included when they materially affect the design.

## Intuition

The key intuition is to ask what information this topic adds to the feedback loop. Some topics add a model, some add a controller, some add an estimator, and some add a test. For **Safety constraints**, the central issue is the behavior summarized above: define forbidden states, operating envelopes, and acceptable risk. The method still has to remain consistent with the plant assumptions and the performance objective.

A practical example is autonomous motion: a planner proposes a feasible route or trajectory, then a tracking controller or MPC layer respects actuator and safety limits while correcting model error.

## How It Is Used

1. Specify the signals, units, sampling convention, operating point, and uncertainty assumptions.
2. Choose the representation that matches the topic: transfer function, state-space model, graph, stochastic model, optimization problem, or certificate.
3. Apply the method to obtain a controller, estimate, trajectory, reduced model, stability statement, or diagnostic quantity.
4. Check the result against the original engineering requirement, including actuator limits, disturbances, noise, and implementation constraints.
5. Validate with an independent calculation, simulation, experiment, or residual check before relying on the conclusion.

## Common Pitfalls

- Test solver convergence, infeasibility handling, scaling, and warm starts at the intended real-time rate.
- Define assumptions explicitly: time domain, sampling, uncertainty, measured outputs, actuator limits, and disturbances.
